What are Natural Resources?

Things that people use that come from the environment. They can be divided into two main categories based on how quickly nature can replace them.

Non-Renewable

Resources that exist in a fixed amount. Once they are used up, they are gone. They take millions of years, extreme pressure, and heat to form.

  • Coal (formed from ancient swamp plants)
  • Oil & Natural Gas
  • Minerals & Nuclear Energy
"They are not replenished on a human time scale. When burned, they extract energy but create harmful pollution."

Renewable (Alternative)

Resources that are replenished by nature within a very short period of time, relying on natural cycles (like the water or carbon cycles).

  • Sunlight (Solar Energy)
  • Moving Air & Water (Wind & Hydro)
  • Plants & Waste (Biomass)
"To remain renewable, a resource must be used no faster than nature can replace it (e.g., replanting cut trees)."

Types of Renewable Energy

Solar Energy

Energy obtained from the Sun's rays. It requires no introduction and can be used for multiple domestic and industrial applications using solar panels.

Hydro Energy

The process of using the potential energy of stored or falling water (usually via dams) to spin turbines and produce mechanical and electrical power.

Wind Energy

Flowing air, created by the uneven heating of the Earth by the sun, is used to spin the blades of a windmill or turbine to generate electricity.

Geothermal Energy

Makes use of the Earth's natural heat from magma (in the form of underground steam and hot springs) to power turbines and generate electricity.

Biomass (Biofuels)

Plant waste, animal waste (e.g., pig poop), agricultural residue, and decomposed material are chemically combined or processed into solid, liquid, or gas fuels.

The Great Debate: Pros & Cons

With global oil reserves on the border of declining, is renewable energy ready to fully replace fossil fuels? Let's compare.

Renewable Energy Sources
Fossil Fuels

Pros

  • Non-perishable: They can be easily replenished by nature. No worry about reserves declining.
  • Clean: No burning of substances that release toxic chemicals or harmful atmospheric byproducts.
  • Plentiful: Available all over the world.
  • Cost-effective: Low maintenance costs. Some (like solar) are easily employed for domestic use.

Pros

  • Historical dominance: Used globally for >100 years. History is on its side.
  • Existing Technology: Technology developed so far is largely based on fossil fuels. Nothing drastically new needs to be invented.
  • Economic driver: Many world economies are dependent on or driven by the sale of fossil fuels.
  • Familiarity: The world has "got used to" them, and people resist change. No other source strongly competes on overall comparison yet.

Cons

  • Reliability: A significant drawback. Efficacy depends heavily on atmospheric conditions and geographical locations.
  • Setup Cost: Initial investment or installation cost is significantly high (acts as a deterrent).
  • Acceptance: Global acceptance on a mass scale is yet to happen. People are not fully convinced about sustainability and value-for-money.

Cons

  • Limited reserves: They are declining and most likely to run out in a couple of decades.
  • Increasing costs: Due to declining reserves and the classic case of demand vs supply.
  • Environmental Damage: Cause a significant amount of pollution, release toxic chemicals, and contribute significantly towards global warming.
